QUESTION-ANSWER PAIR 3

QUESTION:
Based on the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques reference document, answer this question: How do you check rotation?

ANSWER:
To check rotation in the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System, you should follow these steps:

1. **Use the Trial Construct**: Begin by utilizing the trial construct to establish the proper rotational orientation of the components.

2. **Mark the Orientation**: Center the anti-rotation slot (tab) on the anterior aspect of the femur. 

3. **Make Alignment Marks**: Mark the femur both proximally and distally at the points where the anti-rotation slot is aligned. These marks will serve as guides for alignment.

4. **Insert the Stems**: When inserting the stems, refer to the marks made earlier to ensure that the limb is in the correct rotation after the components are coupled together through the intercalary segmental component.

This method ensures that the components are properly aligned and that the rotation is checked effectively during the surgical procedure.

QUESTION-ANSWER PAIR 18

QUESTION:
Based on the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques reference document, answer this question: If the knee does not fully extend, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee does not fully extend, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct in the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System:

1. **Evaluate the Knee Joint Line**: Compare the joint line to the opposite side to ensure proper height.

2. **Check for Distal Positioning**: If the joint line is too far distal, consider recutting the femoral osteotomy to adjust for the discrepancy.

3. **Re-ream the Femur**: Re-ream the femur more proximally to correct the alignment.

4. **Finish the Osteotomized Surface**: Use a calcar planer/bevel reamer to finish the osteotomized surface.

5. **Mark for Orientation**: Use the anti-rotation slot on the trial as a reference to mark the femur for proper orientation when inserting the final implant.

These steps help ensure that the knee can achieve full extension and proper alignment post-surgery.

QUESTION:
Based on the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques reference document, answer this question: What are the choices for the tibial component in the setting of a distal femur fracture? How do I decide which is the best option?

ANSWER:
In the setting of a distal femur fracture, the choices for the tibial component in the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System include:

1. **S-ROM Hinged Tibial Insert Bearing**: This is used when employing the S-ROM tibial tray.
2. **Orthogenesis LPS Hinged Tibial Insert Bearing**: This is used with the M.B.T. revision tibial tray.

To decide which is the best option, consider the following factors:

- **Patient-Specific Needs**: Evaluate the patient's anatomy, the extent of the fracture, and any existing deformities.
- **Compatibility with Other Components**: Ensure that the chosen tibial component is compatible with the distal femoral replacement component being used.
- **Stability Requirements**: Assess the stability needed for the knee joint based on the patient's activity level and the expected load on the implant.

These considerations will help in selecting the most appropriate tibial component for optimal surgical outcomes.

The choice among these options depends on the extent of the fracture, the quality of the remaining bone, and the specific needs of the patient. The surgeon will evaluate the situation and select the most appropriate option based on these factors.

QUESTION:
Based on the DePuy Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques reference document, answer this question: What can I do with the distal femoral component if I can’t close the retinaculum?

ANSWER:
If you cannot close the retinaculum after using the distal femoral component, you can consider the following steps:

1. **Close the Wound with the Knee in Full Extension**: If there is significant soft tissue tension preventing closure, it may be necessary to close the wound with the knee in full extension to alleviate tension.

2. **Soft Tissue Reconstruction**: Ensure that the soft tissue reconstruction adequately covers the prosthesis. This may involve mobilizing remaining musculature to achieve proper coverage.

3. **Layered Closure**: Perform wound closure in multiple layers to minimize hematoma formation and reduce the risk of complications.

These steps help ensure that the surgical site is properly closed while addressing any issues related to soft tissue integrity.
