QUESTION:
Based on the Stryker reference document, answer this: How do you check rotation?

ANSWER:
To check rotation during the procedure with Zimmer Biomet implants, follow these steps:

1. **Establish Desired Positioning**: 
   - Once the desired positioning of the components is established, use an alignment rod to ensure proper alignment with the anterior rotational marking set using the Universal Tibial Template.

2. **Align the Rod**: 
   - Move the alignment rod so that it aligns with the anterior marking. If the alignment rod does not line up, adjust the rotation until it does.

3. **Use the Tibial Cone Reamer Guide**: 
   - The Tibial Cone Reamer Guide has micro-flutes at its base to maintain rotation once set. Ensure that the guide is seated at the same depth/size as the Tibial Symmetric Cone Reamer.

4. **Confirm Reaming Mode**: 
   - If reaming under power, confirm that the drill is set to ream mode before beginning the reaming process.

5. **Engage the Bushing**: 
   - Before reaming begins, ensure that the bushing is fully engaged to maintain stability during the procedure.

6. **Ream Down to the Stop**: 
   - Start the reamer before engaging the bone and ream down to the stop. This ensures that the reamer maintains the correct rotational alignment throughout the process.

7. **Final Assessment**: 
   - After reaming, perform a final assessment of the rotation and alignment before proceeding with the closure of the surgical site.

By following these steps, you can effectively check and ensure proper rotational alignment of the knee components during the procedure.

QUESTION-ANSWER PAIR 18

QUESTION:
Based on the Stryker reference document, answer this: If the knee is too loose in flexion and extension, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee is too loose in flexion and extension, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct based on the Stryker reference document:

1. **Evaluate Soft Tissue Tension**: 
   - Place the knee in extension and assess the soft tissue tension around the joint. This evaluation helps determine if the looseness is due to inadequate tension in the surrounding soft tissues.

2. **Hyperflex the Knee**: 
   - Hyperflex the knee with the patella everted to articulate the tibial and femoral components. This can help in assessing the fit and alignment of the components.

3. **Check Component Alignment**: 
   - Ensure that the tibial and femoral components are properly aligned. Misalignment can contribute to instability.

4. **Adjust Component Size**: 
   - If the components are too large or not fitting properly, consider using different sizes or configurations of the implants to achieve a better fit.

5. **Reattach Soft Tissues**: 
   - If necessary, reattach the vastus lateralis and other relevant soft tissues to improve stability and alignment of the knee.

6. **Consider a Knee Brace**: 
   - If there is grossly inadequate soft-tissue integrity, consider using a knee brace postoperatively to limit flexion and provide additional support.

7. **Final Assessment**: 
   - After making adjustments, perform a final assessment of the knee's stability in both flexion and extension before proceeding with closure.

By following these steps, adjustments can be made to improve the stability of the knee construct and address any looseness in flexion and extension.

QUESTION:
Based on the Stryker reference document, answer this: If the patella is laterally dislocating, what can be done to modify this?

ANSWER:
If the patella is laterally dislocating, the following modifications can be considered based on the Stryker reference document:

1. **Evaluate Component Alignment**: 
   - Ensure that the femoral and tibial components are properly aligned. Misalignment can contribute to lateral dislocation of the patella.

2. **Adjust Femoral Component Position**: 
   - If the femoral component is positioned too laterally, it may contribute to patellar instability. Adjusting the position of the femoral component may help in realigning the patella.

3. **Use a Medialized Patellar Component**: 
   - Consider using a patellar component that is designed to be more medialized. This can help improve the tracking of the patella and reduce the tendency for lateral dislocation.

4. **Resurface the Patella**: 
   - Resurface the patella using a dome patella replacement component, such as the S-ROM or Sigma dome patella component. This can provide a better surface for patellar tracking.

5. **Reattach Soft Tissues**: 
   - If necessary, reattach the vastus lateralis and other relevant soft tissues to improve stability and alignment of the patella.

6. **Check for Adequate Soft Tissue Integrity**: 
   - Ensure that the soft tissue integrity around the knee is adequate. If there is grossly inadequate soft tissue support, consider using a knee brace postoperatively to limit flexion and provide additional support.

7. **Final Assessment**: 
   - After making adjustments, perform a final assessment of the patellar tracking and stability before proceeding with closure.

By following these steps, modifications can be made to help stabilize the patella and prevent lateral dislocation.

Based on the Stryker reference document, answer this: If I can’t get diaphyseal fixation, what are the options?

ANSWER:
If diaphyseal fixation cannot be achieved, the following options may be considered based on the Stryker reference document:

1. **Use of Lock Pins**: 
   - If diaphyseal fixation is not possible, ensure that the polyethylene lock pin is properly engaged through the yoke opening. This can provide additional stability to the construct.

2. **Cemented Fixation**: 
   - Consider using a cemented stem option. This involves ensuring that the cement does not adhere to the hydroxyapatite (HA) coated collar to promote proper bony ingrowth.

3. **Utilization of Extra-Cortical Plates**: 
   - If the femoral component has an extra-cortical plate incorporated into the shaft, this can provide additional stability and support. Implant-grade titanium alloy screws can be used in the screw holes of the plate to enhance fixation.

4. **Alternative Fixation Devices**: 
   - Explore the use of alternative fixation devices or techniques that may provide stability without relying on diaphyseal fixation.

5. **Consultation with Surgical Team**: 
   - Collaborate with the surgical team to explore other fixation methods or devices that may be suitable for the specific case.

By considering these options, you can address the challenges associated with achieving diaphyseal fixation during the procedure.

QUESTION:
Based on the Stryker reference document, answer this: What choices do we have for tibial fixation in the setting of metaphyseal bony defects?

ANSWER:
In the setting of metaphyseal bony defects, the following choices for tibial fixation are available based on the Stryker reference document:

1. **Cemented Fixation**: 
   - The implants can be used in a cemented manner, which provides stable fixation in the presence of metaphyseal defects. Care should be taken to ensure that excess cement is removed from around the implant collar.

2. **Cementless (Press-Fit) Fixation**: 
   - The system supports a cementless option, particularly with porous stem extensions. This method allows for osseointegration with the bone, which can be beneficial in cases with adequate bone quality.

3. **Use of Prophylactic Cerclage Wire**: 
   - In cases of fragile bone, placement of a "prophylactic" cerclage wire around the proximal end of the remaining diaphysis may decrease the risk of intraoperative fracture during the press-fit insertion of a porous stem extension.

4. **Tibial Configuration Options**: 
   - Various tibial configuration options are available, including:
     - **Rotating hinge – Passive tibia**: Suitable for routine cases.
     - **Passive fixed hinge tibia**: Provides stability in cases with marked instability.
     - **Rotating hinge – Polyethylene tibia**: Suitable for routine cases.
     - **Rotating hinge – Metal cased tibia**: Suitable for extra-articular resection or difficult revisions.
     - **Fixed hinge tibia**: Provides stability for knees with significant instability.

These options allow for flexibility in addressing the challenges associated with tibial fixation in the presence of metaphyseal bony defects, ensuring that the surgical approach can be tailored to the specific needs of the patient.
