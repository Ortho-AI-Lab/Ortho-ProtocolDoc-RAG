QUESTION:
Based on the Zimmer Biomet Segmental Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique reference document, answer this question: If the knee does not fully extend, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee does not fully extend in the Zimmer Biomet Segmental Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Hyperflex the Knee**: Start by hyperflexing the knee with the patella everted to allow for proper articulation of the tibial and femoral components.

2. **Insert the Yoke**: Insert the yoke down into the tibial baseplate while the knee is in a hyperflexed position.

3. **Place the Knee in Extension**: After inserting the yoke, place the knee in extension.

4. **Evaluate Soft Tissue Tension**: Assess the soft tissue tension around the knee joint to determine if adjustments are needed.

5. **Closure**: Once the evaluation is complete and any necessary adjustments are made, proceed with the standard closure of the surgical site.

These steps help ensure that the knee joint functions properly and achieves the desired range of motion post-surgery.

QUESTION-ANSWER PAIR 23

QUESTION:
Based on the Zimmer Biomet Segmental Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique reference document, answer this question: When should a distal femoral replacement be used or considered?

ANSWER:
A distal femoral replacement should be used or considered in the following situations according to the Zimmer Biomet Segmental Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique reference document:

1. **Painful and Disabled Joint**: Resulting from conditions such as avascular necrosis, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or traumatic arthritis.

2. **Correction of Deformities**: Such as varus, valgus, or post-traumatic deformities.

3. **Revision of Previous Surgeries**: For unsuccessful osteotomies, arthrodesis, or previous joint replacements.

4. **Ligament Deficiencies**: When there are significant ligament deficiencies affecting knee stability.

5. **Tumor Resections**: In cases where tumors have necessitated the removal of bone.

6. **Treatment of Non-Unions and Fractures**: Such as femoral neck fractures and trochanteric fractures of the proximal femur that are unmanageable using other techniques.

7. **Revision of Failed Total Joint Arthroplasty**: For patients who have had previous joint replacements that have failed.

8. **Trauma**: In cases of significant trauma to the distal femur.

These indications highlight the need for a distal femoral replacement in various clinical scenarios, particularly when significant bone loss or structural compromise is present.
