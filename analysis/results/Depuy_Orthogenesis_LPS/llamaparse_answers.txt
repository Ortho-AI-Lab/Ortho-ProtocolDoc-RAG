QUESTION:
Based on the Depuy Orthogenesis LPS reference document, answer this: How do you check rotation?

ANSWER:
To check the rotation of the femoral component, you can follow these steps:

1. **Manipulate the Knee**: Move the knee through its range of motion to determine the appropriate rotation of the femoral component. 

2. **Marking Rotation**: If the evaluation identifies a rotation different from the previously marked position, make an additional mark or note the rotation relative to the existing mark.

3. **Trial Assembly**: Hold the trial femoral assembly in one hand to prevent rotation while extending the leg fully.

4. **Palpate Vessels**: Palpate the femoral vessels to check the status of the pulse. If the pulse is diminished, flex the knee to see if it increases, indicating a need to modify the length of the prosthesis or remove additional bone.

5. **Final Test**: Perform a final test of the knee's range of motion with the patella in place. Ensure proper patellar tracking and check for distal pulses.

By following these steps, you can ensure that the rotation of the femoral component is appropriate for optimal function.

QUESTION:
Based on the Depuy Orthogenesis LPS reference document, answer this: If the knee is too loose in flexion and extension, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee is too loose in flexion and extension, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Assess Soft Tissue Integrity**: Manipulate the knee through its range of motion to evaluate the soft tissue integrity. If the soft tissues are inadequate, this may contribute to the looseness.

2. **Check Leg Length**: Measure the leg length using reference marks on the tibia and femur. If the prosthetic construct is too long, recheck the length of the distal femoral bone resected against the length of the assembled prosthesis.

3. **Adjust Prosthesis Length**: If the prosthesis is too long, consider:
   - Removing additional bone from the femur.
   - Adjusting the length of the prosthesis.
   - Evaluating the use of a thinner insert.

4. **Evaluate Femoral Offset**: If the femoral offset is inadequate, consider using a longer femoral head to improve stability.

5. **Reassess Patellar Tracking**: Ensure that patellar tracking is proper. If adjustments to the femoral or tibial components are made, reassess the patellar tracking to ensure it remains optimal.

6. **Final Range of Motion Test**: Perform a final test of the knee's range of motion with the patella in place. Ensure that the capsular mechanism can be closed properly.

7. **Consider Additional Procedures**: If necessary, consider additional procedures such as a gastrocnemius flap or muscle transfer, depending on the presence or absence of the capsule or portions of the quadriceps.

These steps are crucial for ensuring that the knee construct is stable and functions properly postoperatively.

QUESTION:
Based on the Depuy Orthogenesis LPS reference document, answer this: If the knee is too tight in flexion and extension, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee is too tight in flexion and extension, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Assess Soft Tissue Tension**: Evaluate the soft tissue tension by manipulating the knee through its range of motion. If the hip flexes when the knee flexes, this may indicate excessive soft tissue tension.

2. **Check Leg Length**: Measure the leg length using reference marks on the tibia and femur. If the leg length is long and the soft tissues are tight, consider adjusting the vertical height or leg length by modifying the segmental trials.

3. **Evaluate Femoral Head Size**: If the soft tissues are tight and the leg length is slightly long, consider using a shorter trial femoral head to reduce tension.

4. **Reassess Resection Levels**: If the segmental trials are at the minimum length and the leg length is still long, consider recutting the femoral osteotomy to adjust for the leg length discrepancy.

5. **Re-ream the Femur**: If the femoral cut is revised, re-ream the femur distally and finish the osteotomized surface with a calcar planer/bevel reamer to ensure proper fit and avoid intraoperative fractures.

6. **Final Range of Motion Test**: Perform a final test of the knee's range of motion with the patella in place. Ensure that the capsular mechanism can be closed properly.

7. **Consider Additional Procedures**: If necessary, consider additional procedures such as a gastrocnemius flap or muscle transfer, depending on the presence or absence of the capsule or portions of the quadriceps.

These steps are crucial for ensuring that the knee construct is appropriately adjusted for optimal function and stability postoperatively.

ANSWER:
If the patella is laterally dislocating, the following steps can be taken to modify the construct:

1. **Evaluate Soft Tissue Tension**: Assess the soft tissue tension around the knee. If the soft tissues are tight, this may contribute to the lateral dislocation of the patella.

2. **Adjust Femoral Component Rotation**: Slight external rotation of the femoral component may aid in improving patellar tracking. Ensure that the rotation is appropriate by manipulating the knee through its range of motion.

3. **Reassess Alignment**: Use the alignment marks made on the anterior cortex of the femur to ensure proper rotational alignment of the prosthetic components. Adjust the alignment as necessary.

4. **Consider Using a Thicker Insert**: If the patellar tracking remains inadequate, consider using a thicker insert to help stabilize the patella.

5. **Reattach Soft Tissues**: Ensure that the vastus lateralis and other relevant soft tissues are properly reattached to provide additional support to the patella.

6. **Final Range of Motion Test**: Perform a final test of the knee's range of motion with the patella in place to ensure that the adjustments have improved stability and tracking.

7. **Postoperative Care**: If necessary, implement a postoperative care plan that may include bracing or physical therapy to further support the knee and improve patellar tracking.

These steps are crucial for addressing lateral dislocation of the patella and ensuring proper function of the knee construct.

QUESTION:
Based on the Depuy Orthogenesis LPS reference document, answer this: If I can’t get diaphyseal fixation, what are the options?

ANSWER:
If diaphyseal fixation cannot be achieved, the following options may be considered:

1. **Use of Segmental Components**: If adequate diaphyseal fixation is not possible, consider using segmental components that can bridge the gap and provide stability. These components can help restore leg length and maintain alignment.

2. **Alternative Fixation Techniques**: Explore alternative fixation techniques that may include:
   - Utilizing a longer stem that can engage more proximal or distal bone.
   - Employing a different type of implant that is designed for less stable bone conditions.

3. **Bone Grafting**: In cases where there is significant bone loss or inadequate fixation, consider using bone grafts to enhance stability and promote healing.

4. **Soft Tissue Stabilization**: Ensure that soft tissue structures are adequately repaired and stabilized to provide additional support to the construct.

5. **Postoperative Bracing**: Implement a postoperative bracing strategy to limit motion and provide support while the fixation site heals.

6. **Reassess Surgical Technique**: If fixation is inadequate, it may be necessary to reassess the surgical technique used and consider modifications to improve stability.

These options should be evaluated based on the specific clinical scenario and the condition of the patient's bone and soft tissue structures.

QUESTION:
Based on the Depuy Orthogenesis LPS reference document, answer this: What choices do we have for tibial fixation in the setting of metaphyseal bony defects?

ANSWER:
In the setting of metaphyseal bony defects, the following choices for tibial fixation can be considered:

1. **Segmental Components**: Utilize segmental components that can bridge the metaphyseal defect and provide stability. These components are designed to restore leg length and maintain alignment in the presence of bone loss.

2. **Intramedullary or Extramedullary Instrumentation**: Prepare the proximal tibia using either intramedullary or extramedullary instrumentation. This can include various tibial cutting guides available with different knee systems, such as the Mobile Bearing Tibial (M.B.T.) revision knee system or the S-ROM® knee system.

3. **Hinged Tibial Insert Bearings**: When using specific tibial trays (e.g., S-ROM or M.B.T.), ensure that the appropriate hinged tibial insert bearing is used. For example, the S-ROM hinged tibial insert bearing should be used with the S-ROM tibial tray, while the Orthogenesis LPS hinged tibial insert bearing should be used with the M.B.T. revision tibial tray.

4. **Patellar Resurfacing**: Consider resurfacing the patella using a dome patella replacement component, such as the S-ROM or Sigma dome patella component, to ensure proper joint function and alignment.

These options should be tailored to the specific clinical scenario and the extent of the metaphyseal defect to ensure optimal fixation and stability of the tibial component.
