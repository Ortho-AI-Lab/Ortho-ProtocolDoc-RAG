Based on the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document, answer this question: What are the choices for the tibial component in the setting of a distal femur fracture? How do I decide which is the best option?

ANSWER:
In the setting of a distal femur fracture, the choices for the tibial component in the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option include:

1. **Standard Tibial Component**: This is typically used for most cases and provides a stable foundation for the limb.

2. **Augmented Tibial Component**: This option is used when there is significant bone loss or deformity that requires additional support.

3. **Cemented Tibial Component**: This may be chosen for patients with poor bone quality or when additional fixation is needed.

To decide which is the best option, consider the following factors:

- **Bone Quality**: Assess the quality and quantity of the bone in the tibia. Poor bone quality may necessitate a cemented option or an augmented component.

- **Extent of the Fracture**: Evaluate the severity and location of the distal femur fracture. If there is significant displacement or instability, an augmented component may provide better support.

- **Patient Factors**: Consider the patient's age, activity level, and overall health. Younger, more active patients may benefit from a standard component, while older patients with lower activity levels may require a more stable option.

- **Surgeon's Preference and Experience**: The surgeon's familiarity with the components and their outcomes can also influence the choice.

By evaluating these factors, the most appropriate tibial component can be selected to optimize the surgical outcome.

QUESTION-ANSWER PAIR 23

QUESTION:
Based on the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document, answer this question: When should a distal femoral replacement be used or considered?

ANSWER:
A distal femoral replacement should be used or considered in the following situations:

1. **Severe Bone Loss**: When there is significant bone loss in the distal femur due to trauma, tumor resection, or infection that cannot be adequately addressed with other fixation methods.

2. **Non-Union or Malunion**: In cases where there is a non-union or malunion of the distal femur that cannot be corrected with traditional fixation techniques.

3. **Complex Fractures**: For complex fractures of the distal femur that involve significant comminution or instability, where standard fixation methods may not provide sufficient stability.

4. **Revision Surgery**: In revision surgeries where previous implants have failed, and there is a need for a more stable and robust solution.

5. **Tumor Resection**: Following the resection of tumors in the distal femur, where reconstruction is necessary to restore limb function and stability.

6. **Infection**: In cases of chronic infection that have led to bone loss or instability, a distal femoral replacement may be necessary to manage the condition effectively.

In these scenarios, a distal femoral replacement can provide a stable and functional solution, allowing for improved mobility and quality of life for the patient.
