Based on the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document, answer this question: If the knee does not fully extend, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee does not fully extend in the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Assess Joint Line Height**: Compare the knee joint line to the opposite side to ensure proper height.

2. **Check for Distal Positioning**: If the joint line is too far distal, consider recutting the femoral osteotomy to adjust for the discrepancy.

3. **Re-ream the Femur**: Re-ream the femur more proximally to correct the alignment.

4. **Finish the Osteotomized Surface**: Use a calcar planer/bevel reamer to finish the osteotomized surface.

5. **Mark for Orientation**: Use the anti-rotation slot on the trial as a reference to mark the femur for proper orientation when inserting the final implant.

These steps help ensure that the knee can achieve full extension and proper alignment post-surgery.

QUESTION:
Based on the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document, answer this question: When should a distal femoral replacement be used or considered?

ANSWER:
A distal femoral replacement should be used or considered in the following situations according to the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document:

1. **Severe Arthropathy**: When patients suffer from severe arthropathy of the distal femur that does not respond to conservative therapy or better alternative surgical treatments.

2. **Severe Trauma**: In cases of significant trauma that necessitate reconstruction.

3. **Revision Arthroplasties**: For patients requiring revision of previous joint replacements that have failed.

4. **Oncology Indications**: In cases involving malignant diseases, such as osteogenic sarcoma.

5. **Primary and Secondary Bone Tumors**: For both primary bone tumors and secondary tumors arising in bone.

6. **Non-Neoplastic Conditions**: When non-neoplastic conditions affect the shafts of long bones.

7. **Failed Joint Replacements**: In cases where previous joint replacements have failed.

8. **Failed Massive Replacements**: For patients who have had previous massive replacements that are no longer effective.

These indications highlight the need for a distal femoral replacement in various clinical scenarios, particularly when significant bone loss or structural compromise is present.

Based on the Onkos Distal Femoral Replacement Surgical Technique: Passive Fixed Hinge Tibia Option reference document, answer this question: What can I do with the distal femoral component if I can’t close the retinaculum?

ANSWER:
If you cannot close the retinaculum after using the distal femoral component, you can consider the following options:

1. **Use Cortical Bone Screws**: If the retinaculum cannot be closed, implant grade titanium alloy cortical bone screws of appropriate length can be used in the screw holes of the femoral component to secure it in place.

2. **Close the Wound with the Knee in Full Extension**: If there is significant soft tissue tension preventing closure, it may be necessary to close the wound with the knee in full extension to alleviate tension.

3. **Soft Tissue Reconstruction**: Ensure that the soft tissue reconstruction adequately covers the prosthesis. This may involve mobilizing remaining musculature to achieve proper coverage.

4. **Layered Closure**: Perform wound closure in multiple layers to minimize hematoma formation and reduce the risk of complications.

These steps help ensure that the surgical site is properly closed while addressing any issues related to soft tissue integrity.
