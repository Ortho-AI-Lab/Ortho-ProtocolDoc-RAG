Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

If the knee does not fully extend, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee does not fully extend, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Assess Soft Tissue Tension**: Check if the soft tissues are tight. If they are, this may be contributing to the inability to fully extend the knee.

2. **Evaluate Leg Length**: If the leg length is slightly long and the implant’s offset is excessive, consider using a shorter trial femoral head.

3. **Adjust Segmental Trials**: If the soft tissues are tight but the femoral offset is acceptable, try reducing the vertical height or leg length by adjusting the segmental trials.

4. **Recut Femoral Osteotomy**: If the segmental trials used are at the minimum length of 25 mm and the leg length is still long, consider recutting the femoral osteotomy to adjust for the discrepancy.

5. **Re-ream the Femur**: If the femoral cut is revised, re-ream the femur more proximally and finish the osteotomized surface with a calcar planer/bevel reamer to ensure proper fit and avoid intraoperative fractures.

6. **Check for Excessive Soft Tissue Tension**: A test for excessive soft tissue tension is to extend the hip and try flexing the knee. If the hip flexes when the knee flexes, the soft tissue tension may be excessive.

7. **Use Trial Constructs**: Use the trial construct to assess proper femoral anteversion and ensure that the alignment is correct.

These steps help in evaluating and adjusting the construct to achieve proper knee extension and alignment.

QUESTION:
Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

When should a distal femoral replacement be used or considered?

ANSWER:
A distal femoral replacement should be used or considered in the following situations:

1. **Severe Bone Loss**: When there is significant bone loss in the distal femur due to conditions such as tumors, infections, or trauma.

2. **Failed Previous Surgeries**: In cases where previous joint replacements or surgeries have failed, leading to instability or loss of function.

3. **Oncological Resection**: When performing a resection for bone tumors, especially if the tumor involves the distal femur and requires extensive removal of bone.

4. **Inadequate Soft Tissue Support**: If the surrounding soft tissues are compromised and cannot adequately support the joint, a distal femoral replacement may provide the necessary stability.

5. **Joint Instability**: When there is instability in the knee joint that cannot be corrected with less invasive measures.

6. **Patient's Functional Needs**: If the patient's functional demands require a more stable and durable solution than what can be provided by other methods.

In deciding whether to use a distal femoral replacement, factors such as the patient's overall health, activity level, and specific anatomical considerations should be taken into account. Preoperative imaging and careful planning are essential to ensure the best outcomes.

What can I do with the distal femoral component if I can’t close the retinaculum?

1. **Soft Tissue Reconstruction**: Ensure that the soft tissue closure completely covers the prosthesis. If significant soft tissue has been resected, mobilizing remaining musculature may be necessary to achieve proper coverage.

2. **Knee Brace**: If knee flexion beyond 90 degrees causes luxation of the hinged insert bearing out of the tibial component base, consider using a knee brace postoperatively to limit flexion to 90 degrees.

3. **Wound Closure in Full Extension**: In cases of inadequate soft tissue integrity, consider closing the wound with the knee in full extension to minimize tension on the closure.

4. **Evaluate for Extremity Shortening**: If there is significant soft tissue fibrosis or tension, extremity shortening may be necessary to allow for wound closure without undue tension.

5. **Use of Drains**: Close the wound over large bore drains to minimize hematoma collection, which can help in managing soft tissue integrity postoperatively.

These steps can help manage the situation effectively while ensuring the stability and integrity of the surgical construct.
