What is the minimum reamer I have to use on the femur and tibia?

ANSWER:
For the femur, the minimum reamer to use is a calcar planer/bevel reamer with a pilot that is at least 1 mm smaller than the last intramedullary (IM) reamer used. For example, if the last IM reamer was 15 mm, the bevel reamer should be 15.5 mm.

For the tibia, the same principle applies: begin reaming with a calcar planer/bevel reamer that is at least 1 mm less than the final IM reamer used, and finish with a bevel reamer that matches the final stem extension size.

QUESTION:
Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

If the knee does not fully extend, what are the steps we evaluate to change the construct?

ANSWER:
If the knee does not fully extend, the following steps should be evaluated to change the construct:

1. **Compare Joint Line**: Assess the knee joint line compared to the opposite side to ensure proper height. The joint line is determined by the level of the femoral component.

2. **Check Segmental Trials**: If the segmental trials used are at the minimum length of 25 mm and the joint line appears too far distal, consider recutting the femoral osteotomy to adjust for the discrepancy.

3. **Re-reaming**: If the femoral cut is revised, re-ream the femur more proximally and finish the osteotomized surface with a calcar planer/bevel reamer to ensure proper fit and avoid intraoperative fractures.

4. **Adjust Tibial Insert Heights**: Varying the hinged tibial insert trial heights can affect leg length. Use the trial construct to assess proper component rotational orientation.

5. **Mark Alignment**: Once proper orientation is established, use the anti-rotation slot (tab) on the trial as a reference to mark the femur, which will serve as an alignment guide when inserting the final implant.

These steps help ensure that the construct is adjusted appropriately to allow for full knee extension.

If the patella is laterally dislocating, what can be done to modify the tibia?

ANSWER:
If the patella is laterally dislocating, the following modifications can be made to the tibia:

1. **Tibial Preparation**: Ensure proper preparation of the proximal tibia using appropriate instrumentation, such as intra-medullary or extramedullary guides. This helps in achieving the correct alignment and positioning of the tibial component.

2. **Use of Hinged Tibial Insert**: When using the S-ROM tibial tray, it is important to use the S-ROM hinged tibial insert bearing. This can help stabilize the knee joint and prevent lateral dislocation of the patella.

3. **Resurfacing the Patella**: Consider resurfacing the patella with a dome patella replacement component, such as the S-ROM or Sigma dome patella component. This can help improve the tracking of the patella and reduce the likelihood of dislocation.

4. **Soft Tissue Management**: Evaluate and manage the soft tissue integrity around the knee. If there is inadequate soft tissue support, consider surgical techniques to reinforce or reposition soft tissues to improve stability.

These steps aim to enhance the stability of the knee joint and prevent lateral dislocation of the patella.

Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

If I can’t get diaphyseal fixation in the femur, what are the options?

ANSWER:
If diaphyseal fixation in the femur cannot be achieved, the following options may be considered:

1. **Use of Cemented Stems**: If adequate bone quality is not available for mechanical fixation, cemented stems can provide stability. Ensure that the stem is properly aligned and that excess cement is removed from the junction between the stem collar and bone.

2. **Intercalary Constructs**: Consider using an intercalary construct that can bridge the gap between the proximal and distal femur. This construct can be secured with locking pins and cement to enhance stability.

3. **Soft Tissue Reconstruction**: If there is inadequate soft tissue coverage due to previous resections, mobilizing remaining musculature may be necessary to achieve proper coverage around the prosthesis.

4. **Alternative Fixation Techniques**: Depending on the specific clinical scenario, alternative fixation methods such as using plates or external fixators may be considered to provide additional support.

5. **Evaluate for Additional Procedures**: In some cases, additional surgical procedures may be necessary to enhance fixation or stability, such as bone grafting or the use of adjunctive devices.

These options should be evaluated based on the individual patient's anatomy, the extent of the fracture, and the overall surgical plan.

QUESTION:
Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

When should a distal femoral replacement be used or considered?

ANSWER:
A distal femoral replacement should be used or considered in the following scenarios:

1. **Bone Tumors**: When there is a need to resect a portion of the distal femur due to primary bone sarcoma or metastatic disease.

2. **Severe Fractures**: In cases of complex or non-union fractures of the distal femur where traditional fixation methods are inadequate.

3. **Significant Bone Loss**: When there is substantial bone loss in the distal femur that cannot be adequately addressed with other reconstructive techniques.

4. **Failed Previous Surgeries**: In situations where previous surgical interventions have failed, leading to instability or deformity in the distal femur.

5. **Intraoperative Findings**: If intraoperative assessment reveals that the remaining bone is insufficient for stable fixation or reconstruction.

In making the decision, factors such as the patient's overall health, activity level, and specific anatomical considerations should be evaluated, along with preoperative imaging studies to determine the extent of resection needed.

QUESTION:
Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

What can I do with the distal femoral component if I can’t close the retinaculum?

ANSWER:
If you cannot close the retinaculum after placing the distal femoral component, consider the following options:

1. **Soft Tissue Management**: Evaluate the soft tissue integrity around the knee. If there is inadequate soft tissue coverage, you may need to mobilize remaining musculature to achieve proper closure.

2. **Suture Techniques**: Use sutures to reattach any available soft tissue structures, such as the iliopsoas muscle or vastus lateralis, to the appropriate anatomical locations. This can help provide additional support and stability.

3. **Knee Positioning**: If soft tissue integrity is compromised, consider closing the wound with the knee in full extension to minimize tension on the retinaculum.

4. **Use of Drains**: Insert a suction drain to help manage any potential hematoma formation, which can occur due to extensive dissection.

5. **Postoperative Care**: Implement a postoperative care plan that includes monitoring for complications and ensuring that the knee is stabilized appropriately, possibly with a brace if necessary.

These steps aim to enhance the stability of the knee joint and ensure proper healing after surgery.

Based on the document titled Orthogenesis Limb Preservation System Surgical Techniques from DePuy, answer this question:

If I have patellar impingement on the mechanism, what are my choices to modify the construct?

ANSWER:
If there is patellar impingement on the mechanism, the following choices can be considered to modify the construct:

1. **Adjust Component Positioning**: Reassess the alignment and positioning of the tibial and femoral components. Ensure that the anti-rotation slots are properly aligned and that the components are positioned to minimize impingement.

2. **Modify the Tibial Insert Height**: Vary the height of the hinged tibial insert trial to see if a different height alleviates the impingement. This can help in achieving better patellar tracking.

3. **Resurface the Patella**: If not already done, consider resurfacing the patella with a dome patella replacement component to improve its tracking and reduce impingement.

4. **Soft Tissue Management**: Evaluate the soft tissue around the knee joint. If there is excessive tension or inadequate soft tissue coverage, consider mobilizing or reconstructing the soft tissues to improve stability and alignment.

5. **Re-evaluate the Bumper**: If applicable, adjustments to the bumper may help in stabilizing the knee joint and improving the overall function of the construct.

6. **Trial Reduction**: Perform a final trial reduction to assess joint stability and patellar tracking before finalizing the implant components.

These modifications aim to enhance the function of the knee joint and reduce the risk of patellar impingement.
